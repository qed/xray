## 1. Department Identity

### Mission
The Sales department drives revenue by acquiring and enabling partners, distributors, ISVs, and manufacturers to deploy and scale payment-enabled unattended solutions that process transactions on unattended machines and generate recurring revenue.

### Scope
- New customer acquisition across all verticals in the unattended retail space (vending, kiosks, EV chargers, laundromats, car washes, parking, self-service industries)
- Expansion of existing accounts
- Building and managing the sales pipeline to hit company targets
- Driving terminal deployment volume
- Managing the full 10-stage sales cycle from discovery through go-live
- Participating in trade shows (monthly, April–December)
- Creating proposals and pricing agreements
- Handling contract execution via DocuSign
- Sending POs to Accounting for invoicing
- Handing off deals to Operations for terminal deployment

### Not In Scope
- **Technical integration work** — determining what application is required, what needs to be loaded on payment devices, what cables and accessories are needed, or what solution a customer needs. These are Products/Technical team responsibilities, though Sales frequently gets pulled into answering these questions.
- **Merchant onboarding** — recently handed off to a dedicated onboarding specialist (as of ~February 2026).
- **Customer support** — should be handled by the Customer Service/Support team, but merchants frequently call Sales directly because they know their rep and because the Support line has long hold times.
- **Integration testing management** — the simulator-to-live testing process should be entirely owned by the Products team, but often gets kicked back to Sales.

### WeVend / MonexGroup Split
100% WeVend. The Sales department does no work for MonexGroup.

### Contribution to 2026 Goals
The Sales department is the primary engine for hitting Wevend's 2026 targets of 10,000 active terminals and $300K MRR. Sales is responsible for winning new partners, distributors, ISVs, and manufacturers, expanding existing accounts, and building and managing the pipeline needed to drive the volume to hit those goals.

### Recent Changes
- **Merchant onboarding handed off** (~1 month ago): The merchant application process and new merchant onboarding was moved from Sales to a dedicated onboarding specialist. Sales still participates in a 30-minute daily status call with the specialist regarding distributor merchant applications, but the specialist owns the process.
- **Account Manager role being created**: Currently hiring (expected within 2-3 weeks) to take over management of all existing accounts, freeing VPs to focus on new customer acquisition.
- **New Products lead assigned**: A new Products team lead will own deals through the technical alignment and solution finalization stages, addressing a major handoff gap.

## 4. Workflows & Processes

### 4.1 Full Sales Cycle (Discovery to Go-Live)

**Trigger:** Inbound lead from website, outbound prospecting, trade show lead, or referral
**Owner:** Assigned VP of Sales, with Oswin involved in pricing/contracting
**Frequency:** Ongoing | **Volume:** 2-4 net new discovery calls per VP per month (6-12 total across team)
**Time:** 8-10 weeks average end-to-end
**Tools Used:** Apollo, LinkedIn, ChatGPT, email, phone, WeSell CRM, Jotform, DocuSign, Excel (pricing spreadsheet), Word/PowerPoint/PDF (proposals)

**Steps:**
1. **Discovery & Qualification** — VP conducts discovery call to vet the opportunity and ensure fit.
2. **NDA Execution** — Customer signs an NDA to enable deeper discussions.
3. **Sales Intake** — Sales completes an integration intake form (Jotform) gathering all initial requirements and passes it to the Products and Technical team.
4. **Product & Technical Alignment** — Products team schedules a technical call with the customer to move integration forward. Product team validates requirements and ensures alignment with the customer's go-to-market plan. *Common bottleneck: technical complexity and Products team not consistently driving this to completion — Sales has to send reminders.*
5. **Solution Confirmation & Pricing** — Sales confirms all fees and commercial terms with the customer.
6. **Contracting** — Sales (Oswin) prepares a standard agreement, adds pricing, and secures a signed contract via DocuSign before any build or pilot begins. *Common bottleneck: 2-3 rounds of legal redlines back and forth. Oswin personally reads through redlines which takes significant time.*
7. **Solution Finalization & System Setup** — Products team defines customer hierarchy, confirms all application builds, and ensures all systems are fully integrated and aligned. Handoff to Sales and Operations. *Common bottleneck: unclear if Products team has a structured process to drive this to completion.*
8. **Testing Environment Setup** — Provide a demo terminal with a test account. Transition the customer from test to live account for validation. *Critical gap: the process for transitioning from simulator to live testing is unclear. No clear test environment. Process often kicks back to Sales or gets skipped entirely.*
9. **Pilot Phase Execution** — Sales works with the customer to run a pilot. Operations supports the pilot including setup and execution.
10. **Go-Live** — Once pilot is successful, transition to full production and hand off to ongoing operations support.

**Inputs Required:** Customer information, technical requirements, pricing terms, signed NDA, signed contract
**Output/Deliverable:** Deployed, live payment terminals processing transactions
**Common Exceptions:** Products team not taking ownership of stages 4, 7, and 8 — Sales ends up chasing. Testing phase is the biggest gap — currently 20-25 merchant applications stuck waiting on QR release because of a testing handoff issue (Sandstar TIDs example). Proposals going through 4-5 rounds of revision.
**Documentation Status:** The 10-stage process is known but not formally documented in a shared location. Integration intake form (Jotform) is standardized. No documented testing process.
**WeVend vs MonexGroup Differences:** N/A — 100% WeVend.

### 4.2 Outbound Prospecting

**Trigger:** Ongoing — VPs prospect daily as core activity
**Owner:** Each VP individually
**Frequency:** Daily | **Volume:** 10-15 prospects researched for every 1 qualified target (~7-10% hit rate)
**Time:** Several hours per day per rep
**Tools Used:** ChatGPT, Apollo, LinkedIn, Google search, company websites, email, phone

**Steps:**
1. VP uses ChatGPT or Apollo to generate lists of potential distributors, manufacturers, and ISVs in the unattended space.
2. VP manually researches each company's website to verify they are actually a distributor, manufacturer, or ISV — this is the most time-consuming step, taking several hours per day.
3. VP identifies qualified targets from the researched list (~1 out of every 10-15).
4. VP emails the prospect.
5. VP follows up with phone calls and email follow-ups.
6. If prospect responds, VP schedules a discovery call.

**Inputs Required:** Target vertical/segment, Apollo or ChatGPT-generated prospect lists
**Output/Deliverable:** Qualified prospects, scheduled discovery calls
**Common Exceptions:** Low hit rate means significant time spent on companies that aren't a fit. No automated qualification or scoring. Each rep tracks their own follow-ups in their own way — no shared system. Prospects have fallen through the cracks.
**Documentation Status:** Not documented. Each rep has their own approach.

### 4.3 Trade Show Lead Generation

**Trigger:** Trade show attendance (monthly, April–December)
**Owner:** Sales team (VPs attend), Marketing leads for exhibited shows
**Frequency:** Monthly (April–December) | **Volume:** ~15-20 leads per show, ~5 convert to discovery calls (~25% conversion)
**Time:** Pre-show research + show attendance + post-show follow-up
**Tools Used:** Claude (exhibitor list analysis), WeSell CRM (lead entry post-show), email, phone

**Steps:**
1. Shows decided well in advance with Marketing, Sales, and leadership.
2. WeVend exhibits at 1-2 shows; attends the rest as visitors.
3. For exhibited shows, Marketing leads all preparation.
4. For attended shows, Sales reviews the exhibitor list — Oswin uses Claude to analyze the list and identify distributors, manufacturers, and ISVs.
5. VPs attend the show and collect leads.
6. Post-show, reps enter leads in the CRM and follow up to schedule discovery calls.
7. Leads that don't convert immediately are nurtured through manual email and phone follow-ups (weekly or monthly, indefinitely).

**Inputs Required:** Trade show exhibitor list, show logistics
**Output/Deliverable:** New leads in CRM, discovery calls scheduled
**Common Exceptions:** No automated follow-up sequences — all post-show nurturing is manual. No formal cutoff for nurturing.
**Documentation Status:** Not formally documented.

### 4.4 Proposal Creation

**Trigger:** Deal reaches pricing/commercial terms stage
**Owner:** Oswin (creates all agreements) with VP input
**Frequency:** Per deal | **Volume:** Low volume but high touch
**Time:** Multiple rounds — 4-5 revisions typical
**Tools Used:** Excel (distributor pricing spreadsheet), Word, PowerPoint, PDF, DocuSign

**Steps:**
1. VP reaches out to Oswin with deal details.
2. Oswin and VP determine pricing, rates, and fees — mostly from a standard pricing schedule.
3. Oswin creates the agreement using a standard form template, adds pricing.
4. Oswin sends the agreement to the customer via DocuSign.
5. Customer either signs or redlines — goes back and forth 2-3 times on average.

**Inputs Required:** Customer requirements, deal terms, pricing decisions
**Output/Deliverable:** Signed agreement
**Common Exceptions:** 50% of proposals are built from scratch (no template fits). Proposals go out in inconsistent formats (Excel, Word, PowerPoint, PDF). Redlines involve pricing, scope, legal, and technical specs — all getting negotiated.
**Documentation Status:** Standard agreement template exists but the overall proposal process is not standardized.

### 4.5 PO and Deployment Handoff

**Trigger:** Deal is ready for terminal deployment
**Owner:** Sales (relay between Accounting and Operations)
**Frequency:** Per deal/order

**New/Demo Customers:**
1. Sales creates an internal PO form on behalf of the customer.
2. Sales sends internal PO to Accounting.
3. Accounting creates invoice and sends to customer.
4. Customer pays.
5. Accounting confirms payment to Sales.
6. Sales sends payment confirmation + deployment form to Operations.
7. Operations ships the terminal.

**Existing Customers:**
1. Customer sends Sales a PO.
2. Sales emails Accounting to invoice customer.
3. Accounting creates invoice and customer pays.
4. Accounting confirms payment to Sales.
5. Sales sends payment confirmation + deployment form to Operations.
6. Operations ships the terminal.

**Common Exceptions:** Sales is the middleman — relaying payment confirmations and deployment forms between Accounting and Operations. Oswin wants this automated.
**Documentation Status:** Internal PO form and deployment form both exist and are standardized.

### 4.6 Weekly Sales Update Call

**Trigger:** Recurring weekly meeting
**Owner:** Oswin
**Frequency:** Weekly | **Time:** 1 hour scheduled
**Tools Used:** Microsoft Teams, OneNote

**Steps:**
1. Oswin preps same morning — sometimes with an informal agenda.
2. Meeting held: each rep discusses what they're working on, wins, blockers, and help needed.
3. Oswin shares high-level updates.
4. Oswin takes notes in personal OneNote.
5. Action items are not formally tracked — "everyone remembers what they committed to."

**Common Exceptions:** Action items slip between calls. Active leads spreadsheet requires Oswin to "pull" updates from reps during the meeting.

### 4.7 Lead Distribution

**Trigger:** New inbound lead arrives (website or other source)
**Owner:** Oswin
**Frequency:** ~1 per week inbound | **Volume:** Low
**Tools Used:** Email

**Steps:**
1. Inbound lead arrives to Oswin via email.
2. Oswin manually assigns to next VP in rotation (tracked in his head).
3. VP follows up.

**Common Exceptions:** If Oswin is in meetings, lead waits. Oswin assigns even when on vacation — no backup system. Rotation is not tracked in any system.

### Bottlenecks
- **Oswin as contract bottleneck**: Every deal's agreement goes through Oswin for pricing discretion and quality control. If Oswin is unavailable, no agreement goes out.
- **Products team as technical bottleneck**: Stages 4, 7, and 8 of the sales cycle depend on Products team taking ownership. They don't always do so, causing delays and forcing Sales to chase.
- **Testing phase**: No clear process, no clear ownership, deals get stuck or kicked back to Sales. Currently 20-25 merchant applications stuck waiting on QR release.
- **Support line hold times**: Causes merchants to call Sales instead, consuming 15 hours/week of selling time across the team.

### Error-Prone Areas
- **CRM data quality**: Reps (especially Rob) don't consistently enter all information. In-house CRM doesn't capture all contacts or activity history. Critical customer data on individual laptops.
- **Proposal inconsistency**: No standard format or template for many deals. Different reps produce different quality and format proposals.
- **Follow-up tracking**: Each rep tracks their own way. Prospects have fallen through the cracks because no shared system exists.
- **Merchant applications**: Incomplete submissions requiring specialist to chase for supporting docs. Delays eat into 3-day SLA.

### Scaling Risks
- **Onboarding specialist capacity**: Already near max capacity with current volume. At 10,000 terminals, the volume of merchant applications will overwhelm a single specialist. The 3-day SLA will consistently be broken.
- **Oswin as agreement bottleneck**: As deal volume increases, Oswin creating and reviewing every agreement will not scale.
- **Manual prospect research**: At higher targets, manually researching 10-15 companies for every 1 qualified target will not produce enough pipeline.
- **Rob's customer management load**: Even with an Account Manager, if the customer base grows 6-7x, account management will need more than one person.
- **Daily distributor status calls**: If the number of distributors increases, the daily 30-minute call structure will consume hours of Sales time.
- **Email volume**: Rob's 200 emails/day will only grow as the customer base expands. Other reps will hit similar volumes.
- **Sales as support backdoor**: As the merchant base grows, support calls to Sales will increase proportionally unless the root cause (Support hold times, lack of FAQ/self-service) is addressed.
- **Proposal creation**: More deals = more proposals built from scratch = more of Oswin's and reps' time consumed.

### Tribal Knowledge Risks
- **Rob Downtown**: All live customer relationships, account history, pricing history, customer preferences — stored on his laptop and in his head. No backup. If he leaves, it's a "disaster."
- **Oswin Menezes**: Lead rotation assignment (mental tracking), pricing discretion, contract review judgment, weekly meeting notes (personal OneNote).
- **Individual rep follow-ups**: Each rep's prospect pipeline and follow-up cadence — tracked individually with no shared visibility.
- **Proposal and pricing history**: Scattered across laptops in various formats with no centralized repository.
