## 5. Workflows & Processes

### 5.1 Recruiting & Hiring

**Trigger:** Leadership identifies a staffing need
**Approval Required:** Peter, Chris, or John G must approve before posting

**Flow:**
1. Hiring need identified → discussed with leadership
2. Job description created (collaboratively with hiring manager/leadership)
3. Leadership approval obtained (Peter / Chris / John G)
4. Job posted to Indeed
5. Rob reviews all incoming resumes throughout the week (~2 hrs/day with active postings)
6. Rob shortlists candidates and sends resumes via email to relevant managers
7. Managers review and provide feedback (informal — no formal SLA, "ASAP" expectation)
8. Formal interviews conducted (interviewers vary by role)
9. Hiring decision made collaboratively with management
10. Offer prepared by Rob using standard template (adjusted per role)
11. Offer approved by Peter / Chris / John G
12. Offer sent via email to candidate
13. Candidate prints, signs, scans, and emails back (no e-signature tool in use)

**Volume:** ~3 active postings at any time | ~2–3 hires per month | ~200 resumes received per posting (varies widely by role)

**Time Investment:** ~50% of Rob's working week is spent on recruiting-related activities (resume review, prescreening, interview coordination, follow-up)

**Pain Point:** Manager delays in reviewing shortlisted resumes and providing feedback. No formal SLA or accountability mechanism. In-person reminders and manager-CC escalations used when delays persist.

**Offer Letter Notes:** ~60% of offers use the standard template with minor adjustments. ~40% require new or custom agreements, sometimes involving employment lawyer review and extended manager review cycles.

---

### 5.2 Onboarding

**Trigger:** Signed offer letter received

**Flow:**
1. Team informed of new hire
2. IT notified of equipment and access requirements
3. Background checks completed
4. Onboarding document list emailed to new hire (checklist needs updating)
5. Documents collected via email or in person on day one
6. First day planned with hiring manager
7. Start date coordinated with new hire
8. Day one: team introductions, policy review, training, login setup (~half day of Rob's time)

**Documents Collected (Canadian employees):**
- Direct deposit form
- SIN (Social Insurance Number)
- Two government-issued photo IDs (front and back)
- Tax forms
- Signed employment contract
- Benefits information and plan selection

**Documents Collected (US contractors):**
- Varies — likely W-9 and contract (not fully standardized)

**Tracking:** No formal tracking system. Rob monitors via email. Documents arrive whenever — no automated checklist or status dashboard.

**Gap Flagged:** Onboarding document checklist needs to be updated and formalized.

---

### 5.3 Offboarding (Resignation)

**Flow:**
1. Resignation received
2. Appropriate team members notified
3. IT informed to revoke access on final day
4. Exit interview conducted by Rob
5. Replacement need discussed with manager
6. Equipment collected on final day
7. Accounting notified of final hours, severance owed, vacation pay owed
8. Accounting processes ROE and final pay

**Terminations:** Handled on a case-by-case basis (specifics not covered in depth this session).

---

### 5.4 Payroll Hours Tracking (Semi-Monthly)

**Frequency:** Twice per month (semi-monthly pay cycle)
**Time Investment:** ~1 hour per pay period = ~2 hours/month = ~30 mins/week

**Flow:**
1. Managers submit approved hours (inconsistent format):
   - Some managers send a compiled list
   - Others CC Rob on individual vacation approval emails
2. Rob collects all submissions
3. Rob manually enters vacation, sick, and unpaid time into TimeQplus
4. TimeQplus data shared with Accounting for payroll processing

**Pain Point:** No standardized submission format from managers. Rob must reconcile across different input styles before entry. Error risk increases with inconsistency.

---

### 5.5 Benefits Administration

**Provider:** RBC Insurance
**Covered:** ~30 employees (Canadian full-time employees only)
**Rob's Role:** Full ownership — enrollment, changes, billing review, deduction reporting

**Flow:**
1. New hire receives plan details from Rob
2. Employee selects plan option
3. Rob enrolls employee through RBC online portal
4. Monthly: Rob reviews RBC billing statement
5. Monthly: Rob updates deductions chart shared with Accounting

**Time Investment:** ~30–40 minutes/month for billing review and deductions update

---

### 5.6 Compliance

**Approach:** Collaborative — Rob anchors HR compliance and pulls in external/internal resources as needed
- Works with internal compliance team on partner and industry regulatory requirements
- Engages employment lawyers as needed for contract updates and legal review
- Maintains health and safety requirements
- Keeps records of all employee documents

**Cadence:** Reactive — triggered by regulatory changes, new hires requiring custom agreements, or partner requirements. No fixed schedule.

---

### 5.7 Employee Relations

**Cadence:** Occasional — not a daily function
**Approach:** Case-by-case. Rob meets with parties involved, discusses situation, identifies appropriate resolution. No rigid playbook.

### 5.8 Office Management

## 7. Pain Points & Bottlenecks

| # | Pain Point | Impact | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Manager delays in reviewing candidates | Slows hiring pipeline, extends time-to-fill | No formal SLA; informal "ASAP" expectation; Rob uses in-person reminders and manager-CC escalation |
| 2 | TimeQplus at 50-employee capacity cap | Growth blocker — cannot add headcount without replacement | Wevend is actively scaling; this is an imminent constraint |
| 3 | Inconsistent payroll hours submissions | Increases manual work, error risk | No standardized format; Rob reconciles across different submission styles |
| 4 | No digital onboarding checklist or tracking | Documents arrive ad hoc; checklist is outdated | Rob tracks via email; no status dashboard |
| 5 | No e-signature tool | Offer process relies on print/sign/scan; adds friction and delay risk | Candidates sometimes delay return |
| 6 | Offer template requires manual adjustment | ~40% of offers need custom agreements; some require lawyer involvement | Creates unpredictable time investment per hire |
| 7 | Cost constraints on tooling | Limits adoption of better systems | Most current tools are free or low-cost; new systems require strong ROI case |
